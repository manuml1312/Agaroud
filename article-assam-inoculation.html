Historical Foundation: 80+ Years of Excellence

The art of agarwood inoculation in Assam traces its roots to 1940, when pioneering researcher Bose successfully sent inoculum to the Forest Department of Assam. This marked the first successful instance of artificial agarwood induction in the state, establishing a legacy that continues to thrive today.

The people of Assam, particularly in the Naharani area of Golaghat district, have inherited these cultivation traditions from their ancestors. Unlike other regions that rely heavily on commercial methods, Assam's approach remains deeply rooted in natural processes and traditional wisdom.

The Assam Advantage: Natural vs. Artificial Methods

What sets Assam techniques apart is their understanding of natural infection processes. In nature, the stem of older agarwood plants becomes infected by fungal consortia through holes made by stem borers. The resulting blackish infection appears inside the stem along the borer tunnel, from which valuable resinous oil can be extracted through water distillation.

Traditional Wounding Techniques

Assam specialists have perfected the art of creating controlled injuries to trees that mimic natural stress patterns. These strategic wounds:

  • Create optimal primary infection sites for beneficial fungal attack
  • Trigger the tree's natural defense mechanisms
  • Ensure controlled resin formation in predictable locations
  • Produce the distinctive "Ghap Mal" quality agarwood prized for oil extraction

Scientific Validation: 2024 Research Breakthrough

Recent 2024 research has scientifically validated what Assam specialists have known for generations. Studies from different sites in Assam have identified specific microorganisms crucial to the agarwood formation process:

Key Research Findings (2024):

Dominant Microbial Communities:

  • Hypocreaceae: The dominant fungal family found in stems
  • Bacillaceae: The dominant bacterial family in both stem and soil isolates
  • Breakthrough Discovery: Using bacterium Pantoea dispersa and fungus Penicillium polonicum, researchers successfully induced agarospirol - the compound responsible for agarwood's signature fragrance
